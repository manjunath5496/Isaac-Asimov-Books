<p><strong>Isaac Asimov</strong>&nbsp;(<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a title="Help:IPA/English" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English">/<span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/&aelig;/: 'a' in 'bad'">&aelig;</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span>/</a></span></span>;&nbsp;<span title="circa">c.</span>&thinsp; January 2, 1920&nbsp;&ndash; April 6, 1992) was an American writer and professor of&nbsp;<a title="Biochemistry" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>&nbsp;at&nbsp;<a title="Boston University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_University">Boston University</a>. He was known for his works of&nbsp;<a title="Science fiction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_fiction">science fiction</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Popular science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_science">popular science</a>. Asimov was a&nbsp;<a title="List of prolific writers" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_prolific_writers">prolific writer</a>&nbsp;who wrote or edited more than 500 books and an estimated 90,000 letters and&nbsp;<a title="Postcard" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postcard">postcards</a>.</p>
<p>Asimov wrote&nbsp;<a title="Hard science fiction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_science_fiction">hard science fiction</a>. Along with&nbsp;<a title="Robert A. Heinlein" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Arthur C. Clarke" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>, Asimov was considered one of the "Big Three" science fiction writers during his lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;Asimov's most famous work is the&nbsp;<a title="Foundation series" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundation_series">"Foundation" series</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;the first three books of which won the one-time&nbsp;<a title="Hugo Award" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hugo_Award">Hugo Award</a>&nbsp;for "Best All-Time Series" in 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;His other major series are the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Isaac Asimov's Galactic Empire Series" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Asimov%27s_Galactic_Empire_Series">"Galactic Empire" series</a>&nbsp;and the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Isaac Asimov's Robot Series" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Asimov%27s_Robot_Series">Robot series</a>. The Galactic Empire novels are set in earlier history of the same fictional universe as the Foundation series. Later, with&nbsp;<em><a title="Foundation and Earth" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundation_and_Earth">Foundation and Earth</a></em>&nbsp;(1986), he linked this distant future to the Robot stories, creating a unified "<a title="Future history" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future_history">future history</a>" for his stories much like those pioneered by Robert A. Heinlein and previously produced by&nbsp;<a title="Cordwainer Smith" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cordwainer_Smith">Cordwainer Smith</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Poul Anderson" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poul_Anderson">Poul Anderson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;He also wrote hundreds of short stories, including the&nbsp;<a title="Social science fiction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science_fiction">social science fiction</a>&nbsp;novelette "<a title="Nightfall (Asimov novelette and novel)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nightfall_(Asimov_novelette_and_novel)">Nightfall</a>", which in 1964 was voted the best short science fiction story of all time by the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Science Fiction Writers of America" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_Fiction_Writers_of_America">Science Fiction Writers of America</a>. Asimov wrote the&nbsp;<a title="Lucky Starr series" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucky_Starr_series"><em>Lucky Starr</em>&nbsp;series</a>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<a title="Young adult fiction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young_adult_fiction">juvenile</a>&nbsp;science-fiction novels using the pen name Paul French.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"></sup></p>
<p>Asimov also wrote&nbsp;<a title="Mystery fiction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mystery_fiction">mysteries</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Fantasy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantasy">fantasy</a>, as well as much nonfiction. Most of his popular science books explain concepts in a historical way, going as far back as possible to a time when the science in question was at its simplest stage. Examples include&nbsp;<em><a title="The Intelligent Man's Guide to Science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Intelligent_Man%27s_Guide_to_Science">Guide to Science</a></em>, the three-volume set&nbsp;<em><a title="Understanding Physics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Understanding_Physics">Understanding Physics</a></em>, and&nbsp;<em>Asimov's Chronology of Science and Discovery</em>. He wrote on numerous other scientific and non-scientific topics, such as&nbsp;<a title="Chemistry" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry">chemistry</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Astronomy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy">astronomy</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics">mathematics</a>,&nbsp;<a title="History" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History">history</a>, biblical&nbsp;<a title="Exegesis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exegesis">exegesis</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Literary criticism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literary_criticism">literary criticism</a>.</p>
<p>He was president of the&nbsp;<a title="American Humanist Association" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Humanist_Association">American Humanist Association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;The&nbsp;<a title="Asteroid" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid">asteroid</a>&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="5020 Asimov" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5020_Asimov">(5020) Asimov</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-asteroid_11-0" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Asimov crater" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asimov_crater">crater</a>&nbsp;on the planet&nbsp;<a title="Mars" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars">Mars</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-crater_12-0" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Asimov_and_Danielson_13-0" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;a&nbsp;<a title="Brooklyn" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>&nbsp;elementary school,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;<a title="Honda" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honda">Honda</a>'s humanoid robot,&nbsp;<a title="ASIMO" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASIMO">ASIMO</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Isaac Asimov Awards" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Asimov_Awards">four literary awards</a>&nbsp;are named in his honor.</p>
